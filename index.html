Habitats

  • Evergreen Forests: These forests are made up of evergreen trees and can be found in a variety of temperatures. In more temperate regions, they may feature eucalyptus, banksia, and acacia trees. The trees offer great shade and protection for tigers to elude hunters and pursue their prey because there is no seasonal loss of leaves.
  • Mangrove Swamps: Mangrove swamps are found along the coast and are created by mangrove trees in slightly salty tidal waters. They contribute to the development of a diversified, animal-rich ecosystem. The Bengal tiger lives in the Sundarbans Mangrove region between India and Bangladesh.
  • Tropical Rainforests: The largest tropical rainforests on Earth are found close to the equator, where they get between 150 and 400 cm of rain annually. They are the most biodiverse places on the globe due to the mix of moisture and temperature. Tropical rainforests are ideal habitats for carnivorous tigers because they are abundant in fauna.
  • Savannas: The savanna receives rainfall only once a year and is characterized by grasses and trees that are spread out so that they do not form a canopy.  This gives the tiger plenty of space to roam and hunt.
  • Grasslands: Grassland areas are the most common in the world, with a continuous cover of grasses and fewer taller plants, such as trees and shrubs. They are home to larger mammals that are perfect to serve as a tigers' prey.
  • Mountains: Mountains are elevated areas of the Earth's crust with steep sides and exposed bedrock. Surprisingly, some tigers live in mountainous areas. A Bengal tiger population was discovered at 13,000 feet in the Himalayas in 2010.

Diet

  • Tigers are Carnivores
  • Tigers are known as opportunistic predators
  • They usually only eat large animals but will sometimes make exceptions
  • Tigers eat between 10 and 25 pounds of prey everyday

Early Life

  • Tigers are born blind
  • A litter of Tigers usually includes two to four cubs but can also include as much as seven
  • It takes two years for a baby Tiger to be capable of living on its own
  • Newborn tiger cubs weigh between 785 and 1,610 grams (1.75 to 3.5 lb)
  • The tiger cubs' eyes will open sometime between six to twelve days
  • Tiger cubs can't hunt on their own until they are over a year old

Personality

  • Unlike other cats, Tigers are great swimmers and like to play in water.
  • A Tigers roar can be heard from two miles away
  • Even though Lions are known as "king of the Jungle", Tigers are actually stronger than them. The reason Lions get the title of king is because Lions tend to have more "king-like" traits and are much more social than Tigers.
  • Tigers tend to hunt alone, although on rare occasions they will share their prey with other Tigers. When there is a group of Tigers they are typically called an ambush or a streak.
  • Tigers can't purr, so they instead show that they're happy by squinting their eyes
  • A Tigers roar is so powerful it can even paralyze any animal that hears it
  • Tigers prefer to stalk and then ambush their prey

Features

  • Like snowflakes or fingerprints, a Tigers stripes are all unique
  • If you shave a Tigers fur, you can still see their stripes
  • Tigers live for about 25 years
  • Adult Tigers can weigh up to 363kg
  • A white Tiger occurs in about 1 in 10,000 natural births
  • Tigers are the largest big cats
  • Tigers are typically nocturnal and prefer to hunt at night
  • Tigers can sprint at over 60 kilometre/hour
  • Tigers saliva is antiseptic
  • A Tiger can jump around 18-20 feet
  • There is a recessive gene in a few Tigers that make the Tigers fur golden, although because the gene is so rare the only golden Tigers that exist are in captivity
  • Tigers can have cubs with Lions. If the Tiger is the mother, the cubs are called "Ligers", if the Tiger is the father, the cubs are called "Tigons"

Population

  • The population of Tigers has decreased by 96% due to hunting, deforestation, and other threats to Tigers
  • In the US, there are more Tigers in captivity than there are in the Wild
  • Three out of the nine species of Tigers are extinct, with another one being functionally extinct
  • Tigers have been around for about two million years
  • There are only about 3,900 tigers left in the wild
  • Around 3,000 of the world's wild tigers live in India
